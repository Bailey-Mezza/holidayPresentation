Things to Do

  • Visit the Tree of Hippocrates: Legendary tree where Hippocrates is said to have taught medicine.
  • Take a boat tour to nearby islands: Nisyros and Kalymnos are just a short trip away.
  • Enjoy local cuisine: Try fresh seafood, moussaka, and local wines at seaside tavernas.
  • Wander through Kos Old Town: Discover quaint alleyways, charming cafés, and boutique shops in the heart of Kos Town.
  • Visit the Archaeological Museum: Located in Eleftherias Square, this museum showcases artifacts from Kos’ ancient past, including mosaics and statues.
  • Experience Kos nightlife: Head to Bar Street or the harbor area in Kos Town for lively bars and beachfront lounges.
  • Take a sunset cruise: Enjoy a relaxing boat ride with stunning views of the Aegean as the sun sets behind the Dikeos mountains.

Paradise Beach

Paradise Beach is one of the most popular beaches on Kos, known for its soft white sand, shallow turquoise waters, and water sports. It's ideal for families, sun-seekers, and snorkelers alike. Don’t miss the inflatable water park in the summer!

Ancient Agora

The Ancient Agora of Kos is a fascinating archaeological site in the heart of Kos Town. Once a bustling marketplace, it now features well-preserved ruins of temples, baths, columns, and mosaic floors. It's a must-visit for history lovers exploring the island’s classical heritage.

Zia Village

Zia is a charming village nestled in the Dikeos mountains, offering stunning views of the sunset and authentic Greek cuisine. Don't miss the handmade local crafts and traditional music!

Neratzia Castle

Built by the Knights of St. John, the Neratzia Castle is a great spot for history buffs and photographers. Explore the massive stone walls, towers, and sea views.

Asklepion

The Asklepion is the most significant archaeological site on Kos, serving as an ancient medical center founded in honor of Asclepius, the god of healing. It's believed that Hippocrates, the father of medicine, studied and taught here. The site features multi-level terraces with temples, baths, and a stunning view over Kos Town and the Turkish coast.

Therma Beach

Therma Beach is famous for its natural hot springs that flow into a rock pool beside the sea. The warm, mineral-rich water is said to have therapeutic properties and is perfect for a soothing soak. Surrounded by cliffs and with stunning sea views, it’s a peaceful spot to unwind after a day of exploring.

Transportation on the Island

🚌 Buses (KTEL Kos)

Public buses run regularly between Kos Town, beaches, and major villages. They're affordable, reliable, and a great way to get around without renting a vehicle. Check local timetables at bus stations or kiosks.

🛵 Car & Scooter Rentals

Popular among visitors! Scooters and small cars are perfect for exploring beaches and remote villages. Bring your driver’s license (an international one is often required). Helmets are mandatory for scooters.

🚕 Taxis

Available throughout the island. Kos Town taxi stand is near the port. Prices are set for common routes (e.g., Airport to Kos Town ~€35). Taxis don’t usually run meters—ask for a price upfront.

⛴ Ferries to Nearby Islands

Daily ferries depart from Kos Town to nearby islands like Nisyros, Kalymnos, and Rhodes. You can book at the port or online in advance. Great for day trips or onward travel.

Local Etiquette & Travel Tips

🚫 Don’t Flush Toilet Paper

Greek plumbing is narrow. Always use the bin provided in restrooms for used toilet paper—even in hotels and restaurants.

💶 Tipping Etiquette

Tipping is appreciated but not mandatory. Round up the bill or leave 5–10% at restaurants. Tip a few euros to taxi drivers, porters, and hotel staff for good service.

🤝 Hospitality & Greetings

Greek people are warm and welcoming. A smile and a “Γειά σας” (Ya sas) go a long way. Shaking hands is common; cheek kisses between friends are typical too.

✋ Avoid the Open Palm Gesture ("Moutza")

Raising your hand with the palm facing outward is considered a strong insult in Greece. It may look like a wave to outsiders, but it's best avoided. Instead, wave with your fingers together or offer a small nod.

🗣 Don’t Assume Everyone Speaks English

Though English is widely spoken in Kos, it’s respectful to start conversations in Greek or politely ask if someone speaks English. A simple “Miláte Angliká?” goes a long way toward friendliness and goodwill.

🍷 Don’t Rush Dining or Service

Meals in Greece are meant to be slow and social. Don’t expect fast service or ask for the check right away. When you’re ready, say “Ton logariasmó, parakaló” to ask for the bill politely.
